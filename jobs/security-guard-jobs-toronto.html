Security Work in the Greater Toronto Area

The Greater Toronto Area has an enormous demand for licensed security guards, driven by the ongoing construction boom, a growing number of condominium buildings, a busy events calendar, and corporate offices that require around-the-clock coverage.

The Etobicoke and Rexdale area is especially well-positioned for security work. Its proximity to Pearson International Airport, the Woodbine Entertainment complex, large distribution centres along Highway 27, and dozens of new condo developments along the Humber River corridor means there are always employers looking for reliable guards. Many of the largest security companies in Ontario, including GardaWorld, Paladin Security, Securitas, and G4S, actively recruit for sites in western Toronto.

How to Get Your Security Guard License in Ontario

Working as a security guard in Ontario is regulated under the Private Security and Investigative Services Act, 2005 (PSISA). You cannot legally work a security position without holding a valid license issued by the Ministry of the Solicitor General. Here is how the process works.

Step 1: Meet the Basic Requirements

  • Be at least 18 years of age
  • Be legally entitled to work in Canada (citizen, permanent resident, or valid work permit)
  • Have no criminal convictions that would prevent licensing (a background check is part of the application)

Step 2: Complete the Mandatory Training

You must complete a 40-hour training program from a provider approved by the Ministry of the Solicitor General. The curriculum covers the following topics:

  • Introduction to the security industry and the PSISA
  • Legal authorities and responsibilities of a security guard
  • Effective communication and conflict resolution
  • Use of force theory and application
  • Emergency response procedures
  • Report writing and note-taking
  • Health and safety awareness

Training can be completed in person or through approved online programs. In-person courses are typically offered over five consecutive days. Costs range from $150 to $350 depending on the provider. Several training schools operate in the Etobicoke and North York areas, making it convenient for Rexdale residents.

Step 3: Pass the Licensing Test

After completing your training, you must pass a written multiple-choice test administered by Serco DES on behalf of the province. The test fee is approximately $66.50. Test centres are located across Ontario, including multiple locations in the GTA. The test covers all material from the mandatory training curriculum. You need a score of at least 65% to pass.

Step 4: Apply for Your License

Once you pass the test, submit your application to the Ministry of the Solicitor General. You will need:

  • Your test results (provided electronically by Serco DES)
  • A recent passport-style photograph
  • The $80 licensing fee
  • Consent for a criminal background check

Processing typically takes 2 to 6 weeks. Once approved, your security guard license is valid for two years from the date of issue, after which it must be renewed.

Additional Certifications That Help

While not legally required for licensing, most Toronto employers expect or require the following:

  • Standard First Aid with CPR-C — A two-day course costing $100–$130. Required by virtually all security companies.
  • Valid G driver's license — Essential for mobile patrol roles and helpful for reaching remote job sites.
  • Use of Force certification — Some employers require refresher training beyond what the basic course covers.
  • Vulnerable Sector Check — Needed for security positions at hospitals, schools, and care facilities.

Security Jobs With No Experience

One of the biggest advantages of the security industry is that many employers will hire you with no previous experience, provided you hold a valid Ontario security guard license. Security companies understand that every guard starts somewhere, and many have structured training programs for new hires.

The easiest entry points for new guards are:

  • Condo and residential buildings — These sites typically have lower risk profiles and provide a good learning environment for new guards. Duties are routine: access control, visitor management, and regular patrols.
  • Construction site security — Overnight and weekend construction site guarding is straightforward work that involves monitoring the perimeter and deterring trespassers. Companies are often willing to place new guards at these sites.
  • Event security — Large events need dozens of guards at once, and staffing companies regularly bring on newly licensed workers to fill these positions. It is a great way to build hours quickly.
  • Retail loss prevention — Some retail chains will hire new guards who demonstrate strong observation skills and a professional demeanour.

To improve your chances as a new guard, make sure your resume highlights any customer service experience, volunteer work, or military/cadet background. Reliability and professionalism matter more than years on the job at the entry level.

Career Growth in Security

Security is not just a starting point — it can be a long-term career with meaningful advancement. Here is what a typical growth path looks like in the GTA security industry.

  • Security Guard (Year 1–2) — Start at entry-level static sites earning $17–$20/hr. Focus on building a reliable track record and learning the fundamentals of site management.
  • Senior Guard / Shift Lead (Year 2–4) — Take on responsibility for coordinating other guards, managing incident reports, and liaising with clients. Pay increases to $20–$24/hr.
  • Mobile Patrol / Alarm Response (Year 1–3) — Transition to mobile roles for more independence and higher pay at $18–$22/hr. Requires a clean driving record.
  • Site Supervisor (Year 3–5) — Oversee an entire security team at a major site. Supervisors handle scheduling, client relations, and incident management. Compensation ranges from $24–$30/hr.
  • Security Manager / Operations Manager — Move into management with security companies. Salaried roles typically range from $55,000 to $80,000 per year. A diploma or degree in security management, criminal justice, or business helps at this level.

Some guards also pursue specialized roles such as close protection (bodyguard) work, cybersecurity consulting, private investigation, or transition into law enforcement or bylaw enforcement careers. The skills you develop in security — situational awareness, conflict de-escalation, report writing, and professionalism under pressure — are valued across many fields.
