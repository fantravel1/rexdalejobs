Forklift Certification in Ontario: What You Need to Know

One of the most common questions from warehouse job seekers in the GTA is how to get a forklift licence in Ontario. The first thing you should know is that Ontario does not have a government-issued forklift licence. Unlike a driver's licence, there is no provincial agency that certifies forklift operators. Instead, the responsibility falls on employers.

Under the Occupational Health and Safety Act (OHSA), specifically Regulation 851 (Industrial Establishments), employers are required to ensure that any worker operating a forklift or other powered industrial truck has been trained and certified by a "competent person." In practice, this training is provided by private training companies that offer standardized courses covering both theory and hands-on operation.

Technically, your forklift certificate is not transferable between employers. Each new employer is responsible for verifying your competency. However, the reality in the GTA warehouse and logistics industry is that the vast majority of employers accept valid third-party certifications from recognized training providers. When you start a new position, your employer will typically review your certificate, confirm it is current, and then provide site-specific orientation covering their particular equipment, warehouse layout, and safety procedures.

You will hear the terms "forklift licence," "forklift ticket," and "forklift certification" used interchangeably. They all refer to the same thing: a certificate of competency issued by a training provider after you complete the required classroom and practical training and pass both a written and hands-on evaluation.

Key Fact: Ontario doesn't have a government forklift licence. Your certification comes from an approved training provider, and most employers accept it.

Types of Forklifts You Can Get Certified On

Not all forklifts are the same, and each type requires its own separate certification. When you sign up for training, you will need to choose which type of forklift you want to be certified on. Here are the most common types you will encounter in warehouses and industrial settings near Rexdale and across the GTA:

Counterbalance Forklift

Most Common

The standard sit-down forklift you see in most warehouses and loading docks. Uses a counterweight at the rear to balance loads on the front forks. This is the most widely requested certification by employers in Etobicoke and the GTA. If you only get one certification, make it this one.

Reach Truck

High Demand

A stand-up forklift designed for narrow aisle work in warehouses with high racking systems. The forks extend forward on a telescoping mast to reach into deep racking. Reach truck operators are in high demand near Rexdale, and this certification often commands higher pay than counterbalance alone.

Order Picker

Specialized

An elevated platform forklift that lifts the operator to the level of the racking to pick individual items. Common in large distribution centres and e-commerce fulfilment operations. Requires comfort working at heights and strong attention to safety procedures.

Walkie / Rider Pallet Jack

Entry-Level

Powered pallet jacks that the operator either walks behind or rides on a small platform. Used for moving pallets across warehouse floors and loading docks. This is often the easiest forklift certification to obtain and a good starting point for beginners entering the warehouse industry.

Rough Terrain Forklift

Outdoor Use

Built for outdoor use on uneven surfaces such as construction sites, lumber yards, and outdoor storage areas. Features large pneumatic tires and higher ground clearance. Less common in the Rexdale warehouse district but used at construction sites and building supply companies across the GTA.

Counterbalance and reach truck certifications are the most in-demand near Rexdale and across Etobicoke's warehouse corridor. Many employers along Highway 27 and near Pearson Airport specifically list these two certifications as requirements or strong assets in their job postings. If you are investing in forklift training, getting certified on both types will give you the widest range of job opportunities and the strongest earning potential.

Step-by-Step: Getting Your Forklift Certificate

The process of getting your forklift certification in Ontario is straightforward. Most people complete the entire process in a single day. Here is what to expect from start to finish:

  1. Choose a training provider. Research forklift training companies near Rexdale and Etobicoke. Look for providers that are well-reviewed, offer the specific forklift type you need, and provide both classroom and practical training. Confirm the course includes a written test and hands-on evaluation, as this is what employers expect to see on your certificate.
  2. Complete classroom training (approximately 4 hours). The theory portion covers forklift components and controls, load capacity and stability, pre-operation inspection procedures, workplace hazards, pedestrian safety, Ontario safety regulations under OHSA, and proper refuelling or charging procedures. You will learn the rules that keep you and your coworkers safe.
  3. Complete practical training (approximately 4 hours). The hands-on portion puts you behind the controls of an actual forklift. You will practise starting up and shutting down, driving forward and in reverse, turning, stacking and unstacking pallets, loading and unloading trailers, and navigating tight spaces. Your instructor will guide you through each manoeuvre until you are confident and competent.
  4. Pass the written and practical evaluation. At the end of the course, you will take a written test covering the theory material and a practical driving test where you demonstrate safe operation of the forklift. The pass rate is high for attentive students. If you do not pass on your first attempt, most providers allow a retest at no additional cost.
  5. Receive your certificate (valid for 3 years). Once you pass both evaluations, you receive your forklift operator certificate on the same day. The certificate is typically a wallet-sized card that includes your name, the type of forklift, the date of certification, and the training provider's information. Most certificates are valid for 3 years before renewal is required.
  6. Get hired and complete employer-specific training. With your certificate in hand, you are ready to apply for forklift operator positions. When you start a new job, your employer will verify your certification and provide orientation training specific to their facility, equipment, and safety procedures. This employer-specific training is required under Ontario law regardless of your existing certification.

Shift Premiums

Many warehouse employers in the GTA offer shift premiums on top of the base hourly rate for forklift operators working less desirable hours. Afternoon shifts (typically starting between 2:00 PM and 4:00 PM) often pay an extra $1-$2/hr, while overnight shifts (starting between 10:00 PM and midnight) can pay an additional $2-$3/hr. Weekend shifts may also carry a premium. These premiums can add up significantly over the course of a year, making a $22/hr forklift position effectively $24-$25/hr on nights.
